Race Guide

The championships, the race weekend format, the scoring system, and the language of the track — everything you need to follow a Grand Prix.

The Two Championships

F1 isn't just about individual glory — it's a team sport with two separate titles up for grabs every season.

🏆

Drivers' Championship

Awarded to the individual driver who accumulates the most points over the entire season. This is the title that immortalises legends like Schumacher, Senna, and Hamilton.

Individual
🏗️

Constructors' Championship

Awarded to the team — such as Ferrari, Mercedes, or McLaren — with the most combined points from both their drivers. This title determines how the sport's massive prize pool is distributed.

The Race Weekend Format

A standard Grand Prix takes place over three days, each with a critical role in determining the race outcome.

Friday

Free Practice 1 & 2

Two 60-minute sessions for teams to test setups, analyse tyre degradation, and gather crucial data about the circuit.

Saturday

Free Practice 3

A final 60-minute preparation session before the critical qualifying battle.

Qualifying (Q1 → Q2 → Q3)

A knockout session where the slowest 5 are eliminated in Q1 and Q2, leaving 10 drivers to fight for Pole Position — first place on the starting grid — in Q3.

Sunday

The Race

A sprint to the finish lasting ~90 minutes. Drivers complete a set number of laps totalling roughly 305 km (Monaco is the famous exception).

⚡

Sprint Weekends

Six times per year, F1 runs a "Sprint" format. Practice time is replaced with a Sprint Shootout qualifying and a 100 km mini-race on Saturday that awards extra championship points.

The Points System

Points are everything — but only the top finishers score them. The bonus point for fastest lap has recently been removed.

Safety Cars & Race Neutralization

When danger strikes — a crash, heavy rain, or track debris — the race is neutralized to protect drivers and marshals.

SC

Safety Car

A high-performance road car driven by a professional leads the F1 pack at a reduced speed. Overtaking is strictly forbidden and the time gaps between all cars are erased — bunching the pack up for a restart. This is the most dramatic neutralization event in F1.

VSC

Virtual Safety Car

For less severe incidents, no physical car comes out. Drivers must immediately reduce their speed by a set percentage and maintain their current time gaps to the cars around them. This preserves the gap structure while the incident is cleared.
