Pit Stop Rules

A pit stop is a choreographed ballet that can make or break a race — often completed in under three seconds.

01

No Refuelling

Since 2010, refuelling during a race has been banned for safety reasons. Cars start the Grand Prix carrying all the fuel needed for the entire 300 km race. Pit stops are strictly for changing tyres or replacing a damaged front wing.

02

Pit Lane Speed Limit

Drivers must adhere to a strict pit lane speed limit — usually 80 km/h. Exceeding it by even a fraction results in an instant time penalty, no matter how good the pit stop itself was.

03

The Modern Pit Stop

With no refuelling, the entire focus is on tyre changes. Modern F1 teams can change all four tyres in under 2.5 seconds — a feat requiring precise choreography from over 20 mechanics.

The Modern Power Unit

F1 engines have evolved dramatically — from massive V12s to today's extraordinarily complex hybrid systems that push the boundaries of what's possible.

1.6L

The internal combustion core of the power unit. Small and efficient by design, it works in concert with the electrical systems to deliver maximum performance.

2026 Power Split

From 2026, power output is split equally between the combustion engine and a massively upgraded electrical battery system — a landmark shift for the sport.

The entire grid now runs on 100% advanced sustainable fuels, marking a dramatic shift away from the traditional fossil fuel chemistry wars of earlier decades.

Active Aero & Overtaking

Aerodynamics dictate how fast a car can corner. The 2026 regulations introduced a revolution in how this performance is deployed.

2026 Regulation

Active Aerodynamics

Cars now feature movable flaps on both the front and rear wings. In corners, they remain closed to provide maximum downforce and grip. On straights, they mechanically flatten out to reduce drag and increase top speed.

Replaces DRS

Overtake Mode (Boost Button)

The traditional DRS system has evolved. When a driver is within one second of the car ahead, they unlock a manual "Boost Button" — delivering a strategic burst of extra electrical power to facilitate an overtake.

Key On-Track Rules

The regulations that every driver must respect during the race — violate them and face the stewards.

📐

Track Limits

Drivers must keep at least one wheel within the white lines that define the track. Repeatedly driving completely off the track will result in a time penalty or lap time deletion.

🔒

Parc Fermé

Translating to "closed park," this rule kicks in the moment a car leaves the pit lane in Qualifying. From that moment on, teams are strictly forbidden from making major mechanical or aerodynamic changes to the car before the race.

🛡️

Defending Rules

A driver may make only one change of direction to defend their position. Moving back to the racing line after defending is permitted, but a second block move is penalised.

The Cost Cap (Financial Regulations)

For most of F1's history, the wealthiest teams could spend unlimited amounts of money, leading to eras of total dominance.

⚖️

The Spending Limit

F1 now enforces a strict budget cap that limits exactly how much a team can spend on car development, manufacturing, and personnel. It is designed to level the playing field between the legendary front-runners and the smaller, privateer teams.

Pre-2021 Unlimited Spending

The wealthiest teams spent hundreds of millions more than their rivals, creating periods of near-total dominance like the Mercedes era of the 2010s.

→
2021–Present Budget Cap Era

A strict ceiling on spending has compressed the performance gap between teams, resulting in more competitive championships throughout the grid.
